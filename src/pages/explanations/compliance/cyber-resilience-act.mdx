# Cyber Resilience Act

The EU Cyber Resilience Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/2847) entered into force on 10 December 2024 and becomes fully applicable on **11 December 2027**. It establishes mandatory cybersecurity requirements for all hardware and software products with digital elements placed on the EU internal market — the first horizontal regulation to do so.

The CRA addresses a structural problem: digital products have historically reached the market with inadequate security, security updates arrive too late or not at all, and users have no reliable way to assess a product's security posture. The regulation shifts responsibility for cybersecurity upstream, to those who design and build products.

Its scope extends into the open source ecosystem in a way no prior EU regulation had attempted, introducing three distinct regulatory roles with different obligations.

---

## The Three Roles

The CRA defines three categories of actor, each with different obligations and a different relationship to DevGuard:

### Manufacturer

A **manufacturer** is any natural or legal person who develops or produces a product with digital elements and **places it on the EU market** under their own name or trademark — whether for payment or free of charge. This is the central addressee of the CRA.

Manufacturers bear the full weight of the regulation: Annex I security requirements, SBOM obligations, coordinated vulnerability disclosure, incident reporting within 24 hours, CE marking, conformity assessment, and 10-year technical documentation retention. Fines reach €15 million or 2.5% of global annual turnover.

→ [DevGuard for CRA Manufacturers](/explanations/compliance/cra-manufacturer)

---

### Open-Source Software Steward

An **open-source software steward** is a legal entity — other than a manufacturer — that systematically and sustainably supports the development of FOSS intended for commercial use. Typical examples are foundations (Apache Software Foundation, Eclipse Foundation, Linux Foundation) and nonprofit entities that govern projects commercially deployed downstream.

Stewards face a deliberately lighter regime under Article 24: a documented cybersecurity policy, conditional incident reporting, and cooperation with market surveillance authorities. They are exempt from administrative fines and cannot affix CE marking.

→ [DevGuard for CRA Open-Source Software Stewards](/explanations/compliance/cra-open-source-steward)

---

### Open Source Maintainer

An **individual maintainer** publishing non-commercial FOSS is largely outside the CRA's scope. Recital 18 states that non-monetised FOSS does not constitute commercial activity. No direct obligations apply.

However, maintainers sit at the origin of the software supply chain. Downstream manufacturers integrating their components must exercise due diligence (Article 13(5)) and are legally required to report vulnerabilities back upstream (Article 13(6)). Maintainers who proactively publish SBOMs, VEX documents, CSAF advisories, and build provenance make downstream CRA compliance significantly easier — without assuming any regulatory burden themselves.

→ [DevGuard for Open Source Maintainers under the CRA](/explanations/compliance/cra-maintainer)

## Comparison

| | Manufacturer | Open-Source Software Steward | Maintainer (non-commercial) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Legal basis | Article 3(13) | Article 3(14) | Recital 18 exemption |
| CRA in scope | Yes | Yes (lighter regime) | No |
| Annex I security requirements | Full | Not directly | Not applicable |
| SBOM obligation | Yes (Annex I Part II(1)) | Not directly | Best practice |
| CVD policy | Yes (Annex I Part II(5)) | Yes (Article 24(1)) | Best practice |
| Incident reporting to ENISA | Yes, unconditional (Article 14) | Yes, conditional (Article 24(3)) | Not required |
| CE marking | Required | Prohibited | Not applicable |
| Administrative fines | Up to €15M / 2.5% turnover | Exempt (Article 64(10)(b)) | Not applicable |
| DevGuard primary value | Automated compliance evidence, SBOM, scanning, advisory publication | Cybersecurity policy tooling, multi-project vulnerability management | SBOM, SARIF, VEX, CSAF, provenance for downstream use |

## Key Dates

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| 10 December 2024 | CRA entered into force |
| 11 June 2026 | ENISA vulnerability database operational |
| 11 September 2026 | Article 14 reporting obligations apply |
| 11 December 2027 | Full CRA applicability |

<Callout type="warning">
    The **11 September 2026** date is the first hard deadline for most organisations: incident reporting workflows and monitoring infrastructure must be operational before that date, well ahead of full applicability in December 2027.
</Callout>
